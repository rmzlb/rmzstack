# Positioning Frameworks

## April Dunford's Framework (Obviously Awesome)

Work through these 5 steps IN ORDER. Each feeds the next.

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives

What would customers do if you didn't exist?

Don't just list competitors. Include:
- Direct competitors (same category)
- Adjacent solutions (different category, same problem)
- DIY alternatives (spreadsheets, internal tools, hiring)
- Status quo (doing nothing)

**Example (TaskPilot):**
- Direct: Linear, Jira, Shortcut, Plane
- Adjacent: GitHub Projects, Notion databases, Monday.com
- DIY: Custom Retool dashboards, manual spreadsheets
- Status quo: "We just use Slack threads and hope for the best"

### Step 2: Unique Attributes

What do you have that the alternatives DON'T?

List every feature, capability, approach, or characteristic that's different.
Be honest: if a competitor also has it, it's not unique.

**Example (TaskPilot):**
- Bi-directional GitHub sync (PRs, issues, commits show in real-time)
- Auto-generated board reports from commit data (no manual input)
- "Zero-ceremony" mode: engineers never leave their IDE
- Built by ex-Stripe engineering leaders (know the pain firsthand)

### Step 3: Value (What attributes enable)

Map each unique attribute to a customer outcome.

| Attribute | Value for customer |
|-----------|-------------------|
| GitHub bi-directional sync | Engineers don't context-switch; adoption rate 3x higher |
| Auto-generated reports | VP Eng saves 8 hours/month; board gets real data |
| Zero-ceremony mode | Engineers actually use it (unlike Jira) |
| Built by eng leaders | Understands what engineers hate about PM tools |

### Step 4: Target Customers (Who cares most)

Not everyone values your unique attributes equally.
Find the segment that gets the MOST value.

**TaskPilot's best fit:**
- VC-backed B2B SaaS, 20-200 engineers
- GitHub-native workflow
- VP Eng who reports to a board
- Previously tried and abandoned Jira

These customers get 10x the value because all 4 attributes matter to them.
A 5-person bootstrapped team? They get maybe 2x value. Not worth targeting first.

### Step 5: Market Category

The category sets buyer expectations. Choose wrong and you fight uphill.

**Three options:**
1. **Enter existing**: "Project management for engineering teams" — buyers know what to search, but you compete on features
2. **Sub-segment**: "GitHub-native engineering management" — you own a niche, easier to win, smaller initial market
3. **Create new**: "Engineering intelligence platform" — no competition, but you must educate the market

## Category Decision Tree

```
Q1: Is there an existing category where your value is obvious?
├── YES → Enter existing category
│   Q2: Can you be #1 or #2 in a sub-segment?
│   ├── YES → Sub-segment the category
│   └── NO → Compete on unique attributes
│
└── NO → Consider category creation
    Q3: Can you afford 18-24 months of market education?
    ├── YES → Create the category. Name the problem, not the solution.
    └── NO → Force-fit into closest existing category, plan to expand later.
```

## Positioning Statement Template

Fill in the blanks:

```
For [target customers]
who [have this problem/need],
[product name] is a [market category]
that [key benefit/value].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
we [unique differentiator].
```

**Example (TaskPilot):**
```
For VP Engineering at growth-stage SaaS companies
who need to show the board what engineering is shipping without manual reporting,
TaskPilot is a GitHub-native engineering management platform
that auto-generates board-ready reports from your actual dev workflow.
Unlike Jira or Linear,
we connect directly to your GitHub activity so engineers never leave their IDE
and leadership gets real-time visibility without asking for status updates.
```

**Rules for good positioning statements:**
1. Specific target (not "for teams who want to be productive")
2. Real problem (something they're actively trying to solve)
3. Clear category (sets the right mental model)
4. One key benefit (not a list of features)
5. Named alternative (makes the comparison concrete)
6. Verifiable differentiator (can be proven, not just claimed)

## Anti-Patterns

**"We're like X but better"** — This positions you as a follower. You win on features today, they catch up tomorrow.

**"We're for everyone"** — If everyone is your customer, no one is. Start narrow, expand after winning.

**"AI-powered [category]"** — AI is a capability, not positioning. What does the AI enable that matters?

**"The all-in-one platform"** — This says "we're mediocre at everything." Pick one thing you're the best at.

**Positioning by founder dream, not customer reality** — Your positioning comes from what customers value, not what you're proud of building.

## Testing Your Positioning

### The 10-Second Test
Show your homepage to someone in your ICP for 10 seconds. Then ask:
1. What does this product do?
2. Who is it for?
3. Why would someone use this instead of [competitor]?

If they can't answer all 3, your positioning isn't working.

### The "So What" Test
For every claim in your positioning, ask "so what?" until you reach a tangible outcome.

"We have AI-powered analytics" → So what?
"You can see patterns faster" → So what?
"You catch churn risk 2 weeks earlier" → So what?
"You save $50K/month in lost revenue" → THAT'S your positioning.
