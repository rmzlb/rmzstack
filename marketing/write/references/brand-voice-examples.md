# Brand Voice Examples

Annotated voice archetypes to help configure your brand-voice-template.md.
Use these as reference points — pick the closest archetype and customize from there.

---

## Archetype 1: The Builder (Technical + Direct)

**Profile:** Founder/engineer who ships. Talks about what's actually happening in
the work, not the meta-game. Comfortable with numbers and specifics.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Short sentences. Often fragments.
- Technical vocabulary when earned
- Self-deprecating about failures
- Skeptical of hype
- Specific numbers always

**Sample paragraph:**
```
"I rewrote our database layer last month. Took 3 days. Probably shouldn't have.
But we were hitting 800ms queries on what should've been 30ms reads, and I couldn't
ship the new feature while carrying that. The rewrite fixed it. Also introduced 4 new
bugs. Two of which I found in production."
```

**Annotations:**
- "Probably shouldn't have" = self-deprecation (honest)
- Specific numbers: 800ms, 30ms, 3 days, 4 bugs
- "Two of which I found in production" = vulnerability (not hiding the downside)
- No exclamation marks
- Short sentences + one long explanation sentence

**Best for:** Twitter, technical LinkedIn, dev newsletters

## Archetype 2: The Honest Founder

**Profile:** Building something and documenting the real version of the journey.
Not a success story — a real-time account, including the messy parts.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Present tense for past events (creates immediacy)
- Admits uncertainty frequently
- Shares the internal dialogue
- Revenue/user numbers as credibility
- Ends with questions, not lessons

**Sample paragraph:**
```
"January 15th. We had 23 users. I needed 100 to justify continuing.
My wife asked how it was going. I said 'good' and immediately felt bad about it.
Fourteen days later we had 97. I spent those 14 days doing things that don't scale —
calling every single user, sitting on customer support for 6 hours a day.
Not because I'd read a book about it. Because I had no other choice."
```

**Annotations:**
- "January 15th" — specific date, not "early last year"
- "I said 'good' and immediately felt bad about it" — vulnerability
- "Not because I'd read a book about it" — authentic motivation vs theory
- Specific: 23 users, 14 days, 97 users, 6 hours a day

**Best for:** LinkedIn story posts, newsletters, long-form threads

## Archetype 3: The Contrarian Practitioner

**Profile:** Has done the thing everyone talks about. Has strong opinions about
why the conventional wisdom is wrong. Backs it with real data.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Opens with the thing they disagree with
- Specific about what's wrong with common advice
- Cites their own data as counterevidence
- Doesn't hedge opinions
- Invites disagreement in endings

**Sample paragraph:**
```
"Growth hackers are wrong about content marketing.

Not 'it takes time to work' wrong. Actually wrong.

The idea that consistent posting compounds is false for 90% of people.
What compounds is quality + distribution. I know because I tested it.
200 consistent posts: 1,400 followers. One post with a real hook + email distribution:
4,200 followers in a week. The math doesn't support the 'just post more' thesis."
```

**Annotations:**
- Short hook "wrong" paragraph gets attention
- "Actually wrong" — commits to the disagreement
- Self-referential evidence: "I know because I tested it"
- Specific numbers for both cases
- "The math doesn't support" — analytical posture

**Best for:** LinkedIn contrarian posts, Twitter hot takes, newsletters with opinions

## Archetype 4: The Clear Educator

**Profile:** Explains complex things simply. Teaches from real experience.
Cares about the reader actually understanding and applying, not just nodding along.

**Voice characteristics:**
- "You" language (addresses reader directly)
- Analogies from everyday life
- One idea per piece (doesn't cram)
- Explicit "try this" takeaway
- Admits when something is hard or requires work

**Sample paragraph:**
```
"Here's the simplest mental model for pricing I've found:

Charge based on outcome, not effort.

Your consulting hourly rate is effort-based. It incentivizes you to work slower
and the client to track every minute. A project fee based on the outcome they care about
fixes both problems.

One client paid me $500/hour for 10 hours. Another paid $15,000 for a 3-day sprint.
The $15,000 project was more fun, more focused, and they were happier with the result.
Same work hours. Different framing."
```

**Annotations:**
- "simplest mental model" — sets expectation
- One-sentence principle: "Charge based on outcome, not effort"
- Explains WHY immediately after (mechanism)
- Real examples with real numbers as proof
- Comparative: both outcomes shown

**Best for:** Educational LinkedIn, how-to threads, email newsletters

## Configuring Your Voice

After reading these archetypes:

1. Which one is closest to you? Pick one.
2. What's different about your voice from that archetype?
3. Pull 2-3 of your best posts and annotate them the same way.
4. Write 5 "voice rules" for your own writing.

**Voice rules example:**
```
My 5 voice rules:
1. Every number is real and specific
2. If I have an opinion, I state it without hedging
3. I write the thing I wish someone had told me 2 years ago
4. Self-deprecation: at least once per post
5. My ending always leaves something unresolved (a question, not a bow)
```

These rules go in your brand-voice-template.md under "Voice Characteristics."
