# Human Writing Patterns

What makes text sound genuinely human. Use this as a reference for the Voice & Storytelling
scoring dimension and as a guide for writers trying to sound less like a template.

---

## The Core Problem

AI writes like a content strategist following a playbook. Humans write like they're thinking.

The difference isn't vocabulary — it's the texture of how ideas connect. AI content is
too smooth. Real thinking is lumpy, contradictory, and occasionally trails off.

---

## Language Patterns That Signal Humanity

### 1. Contractions

AI tends to avoid them. Humans use them naturally.

| AI | Human |
|----|-------|
| "I am not sure" | "I'm not sure" |
| "It is difficult" | "It's hard" |
| "You are going to" | "You're going to" |
| "I have been working" | "I've been working" |
| "We do not know" | "We don't know" |

**Rule:** If a piece has 0 contractions, it's suspicious.

---

### 2. Hedging and Uncertainty

AI is always confident. Humans hedge when unsure.

**Hedging phrases that sound human:**
- "I think..."
- "Maybe it's just me, but..."
- "I'm not 100% sure, but..."
- "Could be wrong here..."
- "I don't know if this generalizes, but..."
- "At least in my case..."
- "Take this with a grain of salt..."

**Why it works:** Hedging signals you're reporting a real experience, not performing expertise.

---

### 3. Variable Sentence Length

Real writers mix lengths dramatically. AI writes in a consistent rhythm.

**AI sentence lengths (too uniform):**
> "Content marketing is important for growth. Consistency matters above all else.
> Building trust takes time and effort. Results compound over the long term."
(15, 13, 14, 13 words — too uniform)

**Human sentence lengths (natural variation):**
> "Content marketing works. Eventually. But 'eventually' is doing a lot of work in
> that sentence — most people quit 3 months before results show up, which is exactly
> when the compounding starts."
(3, 2, 47 words — dramatic variation)

**Rule:** If 5 consecutive sentences are all 10-20 words, it's AI.

---

### 4. Different Sentence Starters

AI tends to start every sentence with "I" or "The" or a transition word.
Humans start sentences in more varied ways.

**Weak (AI-like):**
> "I started posting daily. I saw results after 3 months. I learned that consistency matters."

**Strong (human):**
> "Started posting daily in January. Three months later, something shifted.
> Turns out consistency isn't about discipline — it's about lowering the bar far enough
> that you can't skip it."

**Varied starters humans use:**
- Participle: "Building in public means..."
- Direct statement: "Six months in..."
- Observation: "Nobody talks about..."
- Time: "Two years ago..."
- Place: "Somewhere around month 4..."
- Number: "47% of..."

---

### 5. Incomplete Thoughts (Intentional)

Real writers sometimes trail off or end a paragraph abruptly. It mirrors how thought works.

**Examples of earned incompleteness:**
> "I shipped the feature anyway. Looking back, I have no idea why."

> "We hit 1,000 users. Then 5,000. Then nothing for four months."

> "I quit the thing I was most proud of. That part I don't really talk about."

**Rule:** Every piece should have at least one sentence that doesn't fully resolve.

---

### 6. Strong Personal Opinion

AI hedges on opinions. Humans are direct about what they think.

**AI opinion:**
> "There are different schools of thought on this topic."

**Human opinion:**
> "The 'post every day' advice is wrong. It's right for 6% of people and
> actively harmful for the other 94%."

**Why it works:** Specificity + disagreement signals a real perspective.

---

### 7. Present Tense for Past Events (Immediacy)

Describing past events in present tense creates immediacy. AI uses past tense.

**Past tense (AI-typical):**
> "I was sitting in my office when I realized the product wasn't working."

**Present tense (human-feeling):**
> "I'm sitting in my office. 11pm. The dashboard shows 12 active users — down from 40 last week.
> I know the product isn't working."

**Rule:** At least one story section should use present tense for a recent memory.

---

### 8. Self-Deprecation

AI makes the author the hero. Humans make themselves the cautionary tale.

**AI voice:**
> "Through strategic persistence and consistent execution, I built a successful community."

**Human voice:**
> "I spent 8 months building an audience on the wrong platform. By the time I realized it,
> my competitor had 3x my numbers on the platform that actually mattered."

**Self-deprecation patterns:**
- Admitting someone else was right
- Describing a mistake with specific consequences
- The "I thought X, but actually Y" reversal where Y makes you look dumb
- A decision that seemed smart at the time and was obviously wrong in hindsight

---

### 9. Specific Numbers and Details

AI uses vague quantifiers. Humans remember the specific number.

| AI (vague) | Human (specific) |
|------------|-----------------|
| "several months" | "7 months" |
| "a few users" | "23 users" |
| "significant growth" | "3.2x in 6 weeks" |
| "recently" | "last Tuesday" |
| "some people" | "3 of the 8 people I talked to" |
| "quickly" | "in 4 hours" |

**Rule:** Every claim should have a number. If you can't cite one, say so explicitly.

---

### 10. The Imperfect Observation

Real humans notice small, specific, slightly weird things. AI notices general patterns.

**AI observation:**
> "Networking is an important part of entrepreneurship."

**Human observation:**
> "Every founder I've met in person has been 30% less confident than their LinkedIn
> profile suggests. Including me."

**What makes this human:**
- Specific population ("every founder I've met")
- Specific claim with a number ("30% less")
- Self-inclusion (not just judging others)
- Slightly weird specificity

---

## The Voice Test

Read the content aloud. Ask:

1. Does it sound like one specific person, or could anyone have written it?
2. Are there any sentences you couldn't say to a friend without sounding weird?
3. Do you know what the author actually thinks, or just what they've read?
4. Is there anything in here the author might be embarrassed by?
5. Could you identify the author from the writing alone, even if their name was removed?

**Target:** Yes to 1, No to 2, Yes to 3, Yes to 4, Yes to 5.

---

## Quick Humanity Checklist

```
[ ] At least 3 contractions
[ ] At least one hedge ("I think", "maybe", "I'm not sure")
[ ] At least one specific number tied to a real claim
[ ] At least one sentence under 5 words
[ ] At least one sentence over 30 words
[ ] At least one moment of self-deprecation
[ ] Sentences don't all start with "I" or "The"
[ ] No "It's not X. It's Y." pattern more than once
[ ] Ending is NOT a motivational kicker
[ ] Read aloud: would a real person say this?
```
